Hands-on

Tutorials

Five guided walkthroughs, from your first lake take-off to reading the decoupled force streams. Each assumes you have built the workspace and run source ~/nezha_ws/devel/setup.bash in every terminal.

Convention: open Gazebo from one sourced terminal and run commands / ROS tools from a second sourced terminal. Ctrl-C in the launch terminal shuts the simulation down cleanly.

Tutorial 1 — Your first transmedium take-off

Spawn the nezha_mini transmedium quadrotor floating in a lake and watch it punch through the water surface into the air — the canonical air ⇄ water transition.

1

Launch the lake world

This brings up Gazebo with the lake, the spectral wave field and the vehicle at the surface.

roslaunch nezha_gazebo nezha_mini_lake.launch
2

Trigger the water-exit maneuver

The nezha_mini_wavetakeoff.launch variant spawns the vehicle and runs a take-off through a wavy surface, exercising the Near-Surface-Effect and Transmedium-Resistance plugins.

# wave-coupled take-off (NSE + TR active)
roslaunch nezha_gazebo nezha_mini_wavetakeoff.launch

# or the calm-surface version
roslaunch nezha_gazebo nezha_mini_takeoff.launch
3

Watch the transition

As the rotors spin up, buoyancy hands off to aerodynamic thrust. Because the Phase Switch Console blends the two hydrodynamic models, you should see the vehicle rise without the force jump that a rigid medium-switch would produce.

What to observe: rotor thrust derates as the props approach the surface (NSE), then a brief resistance peak as the body breaches (TR), then clean hover in air with zero water drag.

Tutorial 2 — Triple-domain Husky mission (land → water → air)

Reproduce Scenario A from the paper: the XX-Husky drives on land, dives and tracks an underwater trajectory, then performs a water-exit and hovers as a UAV — all in one continuous run.

1

Launch the all-domain Husky

roslaunch nezha_gazebo nezha_husky_UGV_UAV_UUV.launch

Other staged variants are available if you want to bring the domains up incrementally:

# ground only
roslaunch nezha_gazebo nezha_husky_pure_UGV.launch
# ground + air
roslaunch nezha_gazebo nezha_husky_UGV_UAV.launch
# full ground + air + underwater
roslaunch nezha_gazebo nezha_husky.launch
2

Run the scripted scenario

The mission profile (UGV obstacle run → UUV trajectory tracking → water-exit → UAV hover) is driven from the helper scripts shipped under nezha_gazebo/scripts/.

rosrun nezha_gazebo scenario_run.py
3

Confirm continuity at the boundaries

The PSC monitors the interface and engages the appropriate hydrodynamic / aerodynamic plugins while decoupling ground-reaction forces. In the paper this configuration reached a 100% success rate over 12 trials, versus 10/12 for the UUV-Simulator+RotorS baseline.

Tutorial 3 — Underwater attitude & trajectory control (XX-Mini)

Reproduce Scenario B: a real-to-sim validation where the simulated underwater dynamics are compared against XX-Mini field data (depth error < 0.5 m, pitch error < 2°).

1

Bring up an underwater world

roslaunch nezha_gazebo nezha_mini_lake.launch
2

Start the depth / attitude controller

The underwater 6-DOF dynamics follow the standard Fossen formulation — mass & inertia, added-mass matrix, and linear + quadratic damping coefficients, all read from the vehicle URDF.

rosrun nezha_gazebo UUV_CONTROLV17.py
3

Tune & compare

Because every hydrodynamic parameter lives in the model's config, you can tune the added-mass and damping terms and immediately re-run to see the effect on depth-keeping and surfacing.

Tutorial 4 — Reading the Mechanical-Transparency force streams

NezhaSim's defining debugging feature: instead of one black-box net wrench, every force component is published separately so you can log, plot and compare them.

1

List the decoupled wrench topics

rostopic list | grep -E 'force|wrench|buoyan|drag'
2

Echo a single effect in real time

# e.g. the buoyancy wrench on the main body
rostopic echo /nezha/buoyancy_force

# the transmedium drag (TR) contribution
rostopic echo /nezha/transmedia_drag
3

Plot the water-exit force history

Use rqt_plot (or record a bag) to capture the buoyancy decay, the TR slamming peak and the rotor-thrust ramp during a transition — exactly the curves used to tune feedforward compensators before physical deployment.

rosrun rqt_plot rqt_plot
Why this matters: the same MT interface measured the ~8.5 N force jump of rigid switching vs. the ~1.3 N continuous profile of the PSC — directly from the internal applied wrench, not inferred from trajectory wobble.

Tutorial 5 — Force identification on the gantry rigs

The constrained gantry models hold a body at fixed positions / velocities so you can measure clean force curves — the same setup used to calibrate the LEAP models.

# distance sweep across the air–water interface (quasi-static NSE / TR)
roslaunch nezha_gazebo nezha_gantry_distance.launch

# constant-velocity water-exit sweep
roslaunch nezha_gazebo nezha_gantry_velocity.launch

# drive the rig & log forces
rosrun nezha_gazebo gantry_controller.py
rosrun nezha_gazebo water_drag_test.py

Sweeping the body across the waterline reproduces the quasi-static trans-media test: hold the vehicle at a sequence of fixed heights, read the vertical hydrodynamic force through MT, and watch the PSC produce a smooth, monotonic force curve where rigid switching would jump.
